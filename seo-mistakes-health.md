# 5 Common SEO Mistakes on Health Platforms (and the Correct Approach)

Health websites fall under Google's **YMYL** ("Your Money or Your Life") category, meaning search quality raters and ranking systems hold them to a much higher bar than a typical blog or e-commerce site. A tactic that works fine on a lifestyle site can actively hurt a health site. Below are 5 mistakes specific to health platforms like healthcare.gammal.tech, and what to do instead.

---

## 1. Keyword Stuffing with Medical Terms

**The mistake:** Repeating clinical terms unnaturally to try to rank for every variation — e.g., "Our diabetes prevention clinic helps with diabetes prevention, preventing diabetes, and diabetes risk prevention in Cairo." This reads as spammy to both users and Google's algorithms, and on YMYL topics it's a strong trigger for quality-rater penalties, since it signals content written for search engines rather than for patients.

**The correct approach:** Write naturally for a real reader, and let keyword variants appear organically through synonyms, related terms, and genuine explanation (e.g., "blood sugar," "insulin resistance," "prediabetes" alongside "diabetes"). One well-covered topic per page, using natural language, ranks better than a page crammed with repeated exact-match phrases.

---

## 2. Publishing Medical Content Without Demonstrating Expertise (E-E-A-T)

**The mistake:** Publishing health advice with no named author, no medical credentials, and no indication of who wrote or reviewed it. For YMYL content, this is one of the fastest ways to lose ranking and trust — Google's quality guidelines explicitly call out expertise, experience, authoritativeness, and trust (E-E-A-T) as critical for medical pages.

**The correct approach:** Every health article should show a named author bio, and ideally a "medically reviewed by [Name, credentials]" line with a review date. Link author bios to a credentials page. This is not just an SEO checkbox — it's what separates content that helps readers make safe decisions from content that could mislead them.

---

## 3. Copying or Lightly Rewriting Content from Other Medical Sites

**The mistake:** Repurposing content from WebMD, Mayo Clinic, or similar sites with only minor rewording to save time. Beyond the plagiarism and copyright risk, this produces duplicate or near-duplicate content that search engines will not rank well, since it offers no unique value over the original source.

**The correct approach:** Use trusted sources (WHO, CDC, peer-reviewed journals, Egypt's Ministry of Health and Population) as *references*, but write original content that adds a distinct angle — for example, prevention advice tailored to Cairo's climate, diet, and lifestyle, or advice grounded in local disease-prevalence data. Cite sources transparently rather than reproducing their wording.

---

## 4. Making Unverified Health Claims or Overpromising Results

**The mistake:** Using absolute or exaggerated language like "cures anxiety instantly" or "guaranteed to prevent diabetes." Beyond the ethical problem, this kind of overpromising is a red flag for Google's quality systems, which are specifically tuned to demote misleading or unsafe medical claims, and it exposes the business to real legal and reputational risk.

**The correct approach:** Use careful, evidence-based language — "may help reduce the risk of," "many people find," "according to [study/organization]" — and always include a clear disclaimer that content is educational and not a substitute for professional medical advice. Confident, honest, well-sourced content builds more long-term trust (and better rankings) than dramatic claims.

---

## 5. Ignoring Technical Trust Signals (HTTPS, Site Security, Privacy Policy)

**The mistake:** Treating technical setup as an afterthought — missing HTTPS, no visible privacy policy, no clear contact/about information, or forms that collect health-related data without explaining how it's stored or used. On a YMYL site, these aren't just legal gaps; they directly damage Google's trust assessment of the whole domain, which can suppress rankings even on well-written pages.

**The correct approach:** Make sure the site runs on HTTPS, has an easily findable privacy policy and terms of use, clear "About Us" and contact information, and transparent handling of any patient data collected through forms. These trust signals affect how search engines evaluate every page on the domain, not just the page where the issue appears.

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Content reads naturally — no repeated exact-match medical phrases
- [ ] Author byline + medical reviewer credentials shown on health articles
- [ ] Content is original, not lightly reworded from other medical sites
- [ ] Claims use careful, evidence-based language with a medical disclaimer
- [ ] Site has HTTPS, a visible privacy policy, and clear contact/about info
